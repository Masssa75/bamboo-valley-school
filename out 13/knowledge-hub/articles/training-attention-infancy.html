Training Attention from Infancy

From: Home Education by Charlotte Mason (1906), Part IV, Chapter I

Why Attention Matters

Attention is the supreme habit - "the highest intellectual gifts depend upon the measure of attention cultivated." Compare the lawyer who seizes facts from first statement with the uneducated person's wandering eye and random replies.

"To differentiate people according to their power of attention is to employ a legitimate test."

The Root Problem: Association of Ideas

Without trained attention, the mind is at mercy of "association of ideas" - thoughts jump freely from one to another:

Talk about glass → Cinderella's slipper → fairy godmother → boat → Uncle Harry's boat trip...

Association of ideas is a good servant but a bad master. The person with no power to direct thoughts is at its mercy.

INFANCY

First Lessons in Attention

The Discarded Toy Method

Baby discards a toy → Mother picks it up → Says "Pretty!" with dumb show → Holds infant's eyes fixed for two full minutes.

This is the first lesson in attention. The infant is learning to sustain focus on one thing.

Make Things Interesting

The "butterfly child" flits from thing to thing. Mother supplements by making one thing fascinating:

  • Child glances at daisy and moves on
  • Mother keeps child looking while telling story of "day's eye"
  • The daisy becomes interesting; child attends longer
UNDER 8

Lessons Structure

Lesson Length

Seldom more than 20 minutes for children under 8. No time to dawdle if lesson is properly short.

Timetable

Definite work in definite time. Child knows what to do and how long - this structures attention.

Alternate Lesson Types

Never two "thinking" lessons in a row:

  1. Thinking lesson (sums, reasoning)
  2. Painstaking lesson (writing, drawing)
  3. Thinking lesson (geography, history)

The blood refreshes different parts of the brain when subjects alternate.

Never Let Child Dawdle

  • If child goes stupid - switch lessons immediately
  • Make lessons attractive - know principles of education
  • Don't let child sit dreaming over work
"It is not the things we do, but the things we fail to do, which fatigue us."

The Natural Reward

Work done quickly earns 10 minutes own time. This is presented as natural consequence, not granted as favor.

  • Child finishes arithmetic promptly → Free time before next lesson
  • No praise needed - the reward is natural outcome
  • Child learns: attention saves time, inattention wastes it
GOAL

Self-Directed Attention

By-and-by, the child is taught to bring his own will to bear - to make himself attend. This is a triumph of self-control. The habit of attention, once formed, operates automatically.

"One of the most fertile causes of an overdone brain is a failure in the habit of attention."

Key Principles

  • Start early - attention is trained from infancy, not first school day
  • Short lessons - no dawdling if time is limited
  • Alternate subjects - thinking, then painstaking, then thinking
  • Make interesting - mother supplements by making things fascinating
  • Natural rewards - saved time belongs to child
  • Never reproach - switch activity, don't scold inattention
