The Three-Period Lesson

The Fundamental Montessori Teaching Technique

From "The Montessori Method" by Maria Montessori (1912), Chapters VI & XII

The Three-Period Lesson is Montessori's core technique for introducing new concepts. It comes from Seguin's work with "deficient" children, but Montessori found it even more powerful with normal children.

The three qualities of every lesson: CONCISE (count your words), SIMPLE (only absolute truth), OBJECTIVE (teacher's personality disappears).

"Let all thy words be counted." — Dante
1
Naming
Associate the perception with the name

Show the object and simply name it. No explanation. No elaboration.

"Look at this."
"This is red."
[Show the other]
"This is blue."

Leave the objects under the child's eyes for a moment. Always present at least two contrasting items—never just one.

2
Recognition
Child identifies the named object

Ask the child to point to or give you the object by name.

"Give me the red."
"Give me the blue."

This is where learning happens. Repeat this step multiple times with varied requests.

3
Recall
Child produces the name

Point to the object and ask the child to name it.

[Point to object]
"What is this?"

This is the most difficult step. Only attempt after success with Period 2.

If the Child Makes a Mistake

✗ Do NOT repeat the lesson
✗ Do NOT insist on the correct answer
✗ Do NOT make the child feel he has made a mistake
✓ Smile warmly
✓ Give a friendly caress
✓ Take away the materials
✓ Try again another day

The child is simply not ready. Forcing will "alter the natural state which must be used by her in making her psychological observation."

"To give a ray of light and to go on our way."
— Maria Montessori, on the teacher's role

Apply This Technique To

Colors: Red/Blue, then add more
Shapes: Circle/Square/Triangle
Textures: Rough/Smooth
Sizes: Big/Small/Medium
Letters: Sandpaper letter names
Numbers: Numeral recognition
Vocabulary: Any new words
At home: Everything you want to name
